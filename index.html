Navitas Light Control Unit

The Navitas Light Control Unit (LCU) is an embedded system I designed for Purdue Solar Racing's 9th car, Navitas. The main purpose of this board, as the name implies, was to drive the LEDs on the car. To cut down on wiring and weight, the LCU was designed to be used as three seperate boards. The front LCU would control headlights and front turn signals. The rear LCU would control brakelights, backup lights, and rear turnsignals. The mid LCU would be only used during outreach events, to control cabin mood lighting. Because of the proximity to other auxiliary systems, the LCU also controlled most of the 15V peripherals on the car.

A PIC32 is a bit overkill for a project like this. We chose this specific micro to be used on all embedded systems for Navitas, in order to create more uniformity across each board. This allows for easier debugging and for the programming to be more standardized for all systems on the car.

This page is also mostly standard across all of Navitas' embedded systems. With our previous car Celeritas, there was too much reinventing the wheel when it came to things like the switching power supply.

Here we have the constant current sources for the LED's. When possible, we tried to use the same current source design for each LED. Reliability is one of the most important aspects in racing. When something breaks down in the car, you don't want to be digging around for unique only used once parts, or trying to figure out why one design works and not another design. You want things to be as uniform and simple as possible.

The headlight current source was designed to power two SST-90's. These things are crazy bright, and way overkill for just meeting the minimum requirements. Because we do a lot of testing at night on the car, we needed all our car lights to actually be visible and functional above and beyond Shell Eco-Marathon rules.
